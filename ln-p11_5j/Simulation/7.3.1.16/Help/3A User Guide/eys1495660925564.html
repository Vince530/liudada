Phase detection AF (PDAF)

PDAF works by measuring the disparity between image pairs captured by phase detection pixels in the image sensor. The magnitude and sign of the disparity, correlates with the defocus, and is used to estimate the best lens position for optimal focus.

In single lens reflex cameras, the AF system typically includes a separate optical path leading to a dedicated PDAF sensor. On camera phones, a pixel level design creates an effective aperture for left and right pixels. The left and right pixels, like human eyes, independently capture light. If the light in a scene converges to adjacent left and right phase detection pixels, then there is no disparity and the lens is in the correct position. If the light does not converge on adjacent left and right phase detection pixels, then there is disparity and the lens is not in the correct position.

There are three common pixel design approaches for generating the left and right pixels.

  • Sparse PDAF with metal shielding
  • Dual photo diodes PDAF / 2PD

  • Sparse PDAF with 2 x 1 OCL

Sparse PDAF with metal shielding

Sparse PDAF is the traditional PDAF approach. In sparse PDAF, there are pairs of pixels where each pixel is half-masked so that each pixel receives light through a different portion of the lens. In this approach, 1% to 3% of the sensor pixels are used. On those pixels, a metal shield below the u‑lens creates an effective asymmetric aperture by blocking light at certain interception angles

For imaging purposes, the value of the PD pixel is replaced by an interpolated value of the surrounding pixels, in a similar way to how defective pixels are corrected.

Dual photo diodes PDAF / 2PD

In dual diode PDAF, also called 2PD, each sensor pixel consists of two photo diodes topped by a single u-lens. The position of the photodiodes in relation to the u-lens creates an effective asymmetric aperture. The left and right photodiodes levels are summed up to recreate the regular Bayer pattern

The higher density of PDAF pixels provides several advantages over sparse PDAF including the following:

  • No artifacts (defect) compensation required
  • No light loss

Sparse PDAF with 2 x 1 OCL

This approach is similar to 2PD in that the effective aperture for the L/R pixels is derived from the relative position of the pixels and the u-lens. It is sparse because ~3% of the sensor pixels are used. However, the absence of metal shielding allows the PD pixels to collect more light, making 2 x 1 superior to sparse PDAF with the shielded design.
